Los Angeles Downtown News
Feb. 7, 2005

A Cultural Standoff
Little Panel Defies Big Council Over Police HQ Plan

By Kathryn Maese

Last Thursday morning in a meeting room on Spring Street, the Cultural Affairs Commission went about its routine vetting of designs for lampposts, art projects and fences. Six of the seven members on hand approved a 20-foot sculpture for city Fire Station No. 36. They okayed a tiled mural wall for the Hollenbeck Police Station, and a clay relief for the Canoga Park Youth Arts Center.

The Cultural Affairs Commission surprised many people last week by voting against a City Council-backed plan to build a police headquarters on a plot at First and Spring streets. Said one observer, "It showed a lot of backbone." Photo by Gary Leonard. When Item No. 4 for "architectural submissions" came before the body for a vote, in this case the design of a new police headquarters at the former Caltrans building site across from City Hall, a curious thing happened.

The commission, which has no jurisdiction over where to locate a city building, voted against the Caltrans plan and asked the City Council to reconsider its decision. The low-powered Commission's move was akin to a younger sibling unexpectedly chomping down on its 800-pound brother's hand.

"I've never seen anything like this in all my years in the city," said Brady Westwater, president of the Downtown Los Angeles Neighborhood Council, and one of a group of people at the meeting pushing for a park to be built on the site. "It showed a lot of backbone."

The panel couldn't be accused of ignorance. Deputy City Attorney Frank Orozco clearly warned that the full Council would overturn the decision, and the motion would be interpreted as a negative act.

"I don't think it will have much weight," he said.

Whether they were swayed by the crowd or there was something else at work, the Commission members ignored Orozco and instead listened to those who came to plead for a different Downtown. Following a heated 90-minute discussion, the commission voted 5-1 in favor of a motion asking the City Council to "reconsider the appropriateness of the building on this site." They made the decision on the grounds that it is "harmful to the cultural life of Downtown," particularly the nascent residential and arts community springing up in the area. A strongly worded letter will be sent to Council members in the coming weeks.

"If the question is if the building is well designed for the site, yes it is," said Commissioner Charles Stern, who proposed the motion. "I still think it's a serious mistake to put the building through."

The reaction to the vote was mixed, and somewhat confused following the legal wrangling over rules of order required for the motion.

"I think we are feeling like we're the court of last resort," said commissioner Lee Ramer, referring to the nearly two dozen people who filled the 10th floor room to plead their case. A group of new Downtown residents argued for a park on the site. Others called for a police station.

The debate over the half-block site at First and Spring streets is not a new one. For more than a decade the city weighed the merit of various sites for a new police structure to replace the earthquake-damaged Parker Center. Last June, Councilwoman Jan Perry and then-Chief Legislative Analyst Ron Deaton came up with the vacant Caltrans site. Most high-level players quickly signed on and it appeared as if the contentious issue had finally been resolved.

Now, as the Council pushes forward with plans, the Cultural Affairs Commission stands in the way - at least temporarily. The Council will have to revisit the issue, which is what the commission wanted. Theoretically, they could reopen a public comment process that some charge was initially flawed.

"What we don't understand is why city officials rushed to identify this site without public conversation," said John Agnew, a member of lacivicpark.org, a community group that has collected more than 1,000 signatures in favor of a park. Agnew, and others at the meeting, said the council's June 23 vote in support of the police headquarters came as an amendment to another motion removing a property at First and Alameda streets from consideration. They were upset by what they said was a lack of proper notification and discussion about the Caltrans site.

The commission also questioned whether the City Council had sufficiently considered a 1997 master plan for the Civic Center that identified the Caltrans block as a prime candidate for open space. Six weeks before the final vote on the police headquarters, Perry created an advisory committee to come up with concepts for a possible park.

"It would be foolhardy and sad if the master plan was not referenced," said commissioner Paula Holt, who argued that the panel's vote on the architecture could not be separated from the location.

There were plenty of proponents of the police headquarters at the Caltrans location, including Chris Espinoza, a representative from the Chief Legislative Analyst's office.

"This process was reviewed extensively through public committees," Espinoza said. "The idea that this was an amendment and was rushed through is untrue. Many people worked extensively on it."

Several groups representing businesses and residents in Little Tokyo also voiced their support for the headquarters. They had lobbied Perry and the Council hard to keep the project from being built at First and Alameda, which many argued should be used to help redevelop the district.

As it stands now, plans call for demolishing the former Caltrans structure and building an 11-story police replacement headquarters - with a small park around the perimeter. It will be interesting to see what the 800-pound brother does next.
