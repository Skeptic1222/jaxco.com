Re: Comment on Police Headquarters Facility Plan, Initial Study
Comment on Prop. Q & F Civic Center Public Safety Facilities, Draft EIR

Dear Ms. Ochsner:

Our group, lacivicpark.org, supports your Initial Study finding that the Police Headquarters Facility Plan requires a full Environmental Impact Report. We believe that the proposed Police Headquarters Facility on the old Caltrans and St. Vibiana’s blocks would have significant and permanent negative impacts on aesthetics, air quality, noise, traffic and cultural resources.

We are a coalition of public space advocates, downtown residents, businesses, and others from throughout the city in support of a major civic park for Los Angeles on the old Caltrans site. More than 1,000 people have joined us in support of that goal. We believe the alternative use of the block as public open space needs to be acknowledged in evaluating the environmental impact of the PHF proposal -- it is a use of the site that would have dramatic positive environmental impact.

Among the points requiring further study:

Cultural resources -- There is a significant emerging arts and cultural development in the immediate area. Those include the just-completed $6 million restoration and redevelopment of the former St. Vibiana’s Cathedral into a performing arts venue; Gallery Row, beginning at 2nd Street and extending along Spring and Main, which has grown in one year from four galleries to more than a dozen; the just-beginning restoration and redevelopment of the historic Linda Lea Theater on Main into a film and performance venue. These initiatives and their connection to the larger arts community, including the museums and performing arts centers on Grand Avenue and in Little Tokyo, will be physically and psychologically divided by the police project. St. Vibiana’s will be trapped at an intersection that bears the full brunt of the congestion the headquarters and its parking facilities will create. On Main Street, the MJ Higgins gallery – where the Gallery Row project was born – would literally be torn down. Instead of a place honoring creative and artistic energy, the site would house police parking and a motor pool where police vehicles are repaired, washed and refueled. The almost certain disruption and undermining of these arts and cultural endeavors has not been acknowledged in the Initial Study.

Historic and architectural value -- The site is adjacent to some of the city’s greatest architectural landmarks – including City Hall (1928), St. Vibiana’s (1876), the LA Times (1935), and new Caltrans building (2004). St. Vibiana’s, narrowly saved from an earlier wrecking ball, was the center of the Catholic community in Los Angles for more than a century. The Times has chronicled city, national and world events since 1881. City Hall is an icon of the city itself and a symbolic beacon of hope. Placing a police headquarters in the midst of these buildings is incompatible with their cultural legacies and will wall off City Hall from the natural gateway to its citizens and historic districts.
The old Caltrans site itself contains two buildings of note, both scheduled to be demolished: the main Caltrans building, constructed in the 1930s and 1940s, and the Wilson Building, which dates to 1889. Targeted for demolition on the St. Vibiana’s block is the1896 building that houses the MJ Higgins Gallery. The value of these buildings has been given cursory mention in the Initial Report. Each demands careful evaluation before the irreversible action of destroying them is taken.

Residential Area – This project on this site is incompatible with a rapidly expanding residential community. Directly adjacent to the site is the Higgins Building, with 135 units and more than 200 residents. In the surrounding blocks, the residential population will grow by thousands in the next few years – within the time frame the proposed police project would be completed. The creation of housing in reclaimed historic buildings and in newly-constructed buildings is part of a larger city-backed movement to expand the housing pool and to rebuild downtown. There are many existing and coming residential projects in the Old Bank District, along Spring, Main and Broadway, on Bunker Hill, in Little Tokyo and in the Artists and Toy Factory districts. Even closer to the site, new housing construction is planned for the St. Vibiana’s block and the block to the southeast, bordered by 2nd, 3rd, Los Angeles and San Pedro streets. The rehabbed Douglas Building, one block away at Spring and 3rd, is among a number that will be opening very shortly. In evaluating this project, a full accounting of existing projects and those in development must be made and taken into account.

Children -- Currently, there are children living in the Higgins and other residential buildings and there are child daycare centers at the new Caltrans building to the east and at the Ronald Reagan Office Building to the south. As the general population of downtown increases, so will its population of children and the need for parks and other health-friendly environments. The impact of the project on chidlren -- who are especially vulnerable to air pollution and other negative environmental influences – requires specific analysis.

Workplace Impact -- Thousands of workers are concentrated in the immediate neighboring buildings, including those who work in City Hall, the new Caltrans building, the LA Times, the Higgins Building and the Recycler building. The noise, air pollution and traffic congestion from a police headquarters on this site will negatively impact those workplace environments and others in the area.

Helipad -- Significant impact will result from the addition of a helipad in close proximity to residential areas. The disruptive flight patterns and unfettered use, especially at nighttime and early morning, will adversely affect the quiet enjoyment of neighboring residences. The nuisance factor of helipad operation must be further explored and proper mitigation measures employed.

Traffic -- The Initial Study unnecessarily limits the range of air quality impacts
from the PHF. Not only are short-term air quality impacts likely to result from the construction in a heavily urbanized area, long-term impacts will be significant. For example, the PHF proposes to build parking structures with 1,200 new parking spaces for the police fleet. This is a significant increase in traffic and emissions over the previous use. In addition to the police fleet, the PHF will attract traffic from employees and civilians, resulting in a further amassing of vehicle emissions within a well-defined area. If the primary entrances for the facilities and the motor pool are all on Main Street – as shown in preliminary drawings – the traffic concentrations will be dramatically higher than indicated.

Auto Garage - The Initial Study inappropriately overlooks the operational impacts of an auto repair shop with services bays, car wash and fuel island within the MTD. This facility is designed to service 1,000 police vehicles. The auto repair shop, service bays and fuel islands will likely require a series of Permits to Operate from the South Coast Air Quality Management District and could potentially implicate the Resources Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976. In addition, the odors and pollutants emanating from these facilities, in close proximity to residences with elderly and children, will significantly affect a substantial number of people. This concern is heightened by the foreseeable construction of residential units in the areas surrounding the proposed projects and foreseeable conversion of nearby buildings into lofts.

Parking -- The Initial Study inappropriately combines the parking impacts of all
proposed projects, thereby reducing the significance of any single one. The PHF has the potential to drastically affect public parking availability in the areas immediately adjacent to the facility by the removal of public curbside and surface lot parking spaces and its use as a center of police administrative activity. These impacts are significant in the areas immediately adjacent to the PHF and cannot be minimized by inappropriately combining the aggregate parking over an expansive geographical area.

Archeological Resources -- The EIR must adequately survey the proposed
project area for archeological resources, paleontological resources, and
human remains that may be irreparably harmed by construction of the below grade parking structures. The area presently known as Downtown Los Angeles served as a center for agricultural and civic activity prior to modern-day development. There exists a high potentiality that archeological resources, paleontological resources, and human remains are located within the project areas, and this potential is increased by the fact that the parking structures are to be constructed well below grade. In the event archeological resources, paleontological resources, and human remains are discovered during construction activities for any and all proposed projects, mitigation is required and standard construction practices must be employed.

Hazardous Materials – Due to the age of the edifices, it is likely that demolition activities will result in the release of asbestos-containing materials and lead-based paint dust. The EIR must identify measures for the control and handling of these hazardous materials and employ proper mitigation measures, such as total enclosure and capture during demolition, to ensure that the harmful materials do
not escape to nearby residences and offices.

Hazards – The security measures being taken for the headquarters and its parking facilities underscore its potential to be an inviting target for those who would do harm. To place such a facility in immediate proximity to so many residences and workplaces – as well as the seat of city government and its emergency command center -- suggests an unacceptable public safety compromise for which there can be no mitigation.

Conflict with Applicable Land Use Plans, Policies or Regulations – In 1997, The Civic Center Shared Facilities and Enhancement Plan (also known as the 10-Minute Diamond Plan), was prepared and approved by the L.A. Civic Center Authority, acting on instructions from the LA City Council and the LA County Board of Supervisors. The plan, which calls for the block to be developed as open space, was formally adopted by the City Council in December 1997 as the master plan for the Civic Center. To quote from the master plan: “The Civic Square can serve as the meeting place of the public realm and the private sector. Where City Hall can share the backdrop of a common space along with business, the press, other branches of government, and cultural and religious institutions. It is a place for bringing the people of the City together. It is a park for everyday life, the gathering place for extraordinary events and a symbol of civic life in all its diversity and complexity.”
In 2000 and 2001, when the Council approved the land swap that gives LA ownership of this block, its motions repeatedly talked of the opportunity for open space. The swap would allow the city to use the “property directly across from the First Street entrance to City Hall … for added green space in the Civic Center area.” Private property on the block was subject to condemnation because “the public interest and necessity require the acquisition of real property for the creation of open space.” Once the new Caltrans building is completed, the Council said, the city will “convert the block directly across from the First Street Steps of City Hall into open space.”

Open space – The Initial Study presumes that the old Caltrans site exists as a cleared and open space. If that is indeed how the site must be evaluated, then we would argue that to build on the site will result in a tremendous loss of open space in the heart of downtown. It is open space with dramatic vistas of the mountains and surrounding landmarks.

Alternatives -- The PHF must be subject to an honest and adequate alternatives assessment. Under CEQA Guidelines, a draft EIR must "describe a range of reasonable alternatives to the project, or to the location of the project that could feasibly attain most of the basic objectives of the project but would avoid or substantially lessen any of the significant effects of the project, and evaluate the comparative merits of the alternatives." CEQA Guidelines, Section 15126.6(a). In
considering an "off-site" or alternative location for the proposed project, the lead agency must consider whether any of the significant effects of the project would be avoided or substantially lessened by putting the project in another location. CEQA Guidelines, Section 15126.6(f)(2)(A). A cursory alternatives assessment used to justify a pre-determined decision will not suffice under CEQA.
It must be noted that the Civic Center master plan from 1997 anticipated the need for a new police HQ and other public safety facilities and located them at the current Parker Center site. The plan calls for constructing a new police administrative building (PAB) and fire administrative building (FAB) prior to demolishing Parker Center and then to build a garage and an Emergency Operations Center on the site – and still have a future development site for City use. All of that was to be accomplished without relocating the police department during construction. It is a plan that must be considered in evaluating Parker Center as an alternative site.

In closing, we would note that objections to this project and the manner in which the site was chosen by the City Council have been registered from many quarters – including residential, businesses, arts and cultural organizations. Significantly, it has been red-flagged by the city’s Cultural Affairs Commission, which has the responsibility for approving all architectural proposals on city property: it voted Jan. 20 against approving the plans for the PHF on the site. And, on Feb. 3, the commission passed a motion asking the City Council to revisit the subject of the appropriateness of the PHF for the site.

On behalf of lacivicpark.org, we ask that you consider these points as you further evaluate this important matter.
