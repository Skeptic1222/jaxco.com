James Rojas in an open space advocate and urban planner in Los Angeles. He is co-owner of Gallery 727, a founder of the Latino Urban Forum and resident of downtown who grew up in East L.A. The following is based on remarks he made at the “Grassroots Park Party,” Nov 10, 2004, in support of a civic park in Downtown L.A.

Why L.A. Needs a Civic Park
By James Rojas

All over Los Angeles residents are fighting to create open space and a sense of community. Downtown L.A. is no exception.

I am passionate about making places where people can come together.

A few years ago a few friends and I started the Latino Urban Forum with the mission to improve the built environment of Latino Communities. What makes the Latino Urban Forum successful is our innovative approach to improving communities through the public realm. Good public spaces make good communities.

LUF has worked on a wide variety of projects from pedestrian improvements to lobbying for state parks. Our work involves community organizing to project development.

The Cornfields State Park (Zanja Madre State Park) is a 32-acre former freight yard between the L.A. River/Chinatown that will be turned into a park. LUF was part of the Chinatown Alliance, a group of local community groups and environmentalist, which fought against the city’s plan to build warehouses on the site.

Evergreen Cemetery Jogging Path is an innovative, urban/recreational space project in Boyle Heights conceived by the LUF in collaboration with neighborhood residents to convert 1.5 miles of city sidewalk into a rubberized jogging path. Today over 1,000 joggers/walkers a day use this facility.

Northeast Open Space Coalition is an organization the Latino Urban Forum help create with
residents in North East Los Angeles communities to promote, protect and enhance open space.

South Central Farmers is a 14-acre community garden at 41st and Alameda streets where the city wants to build warehouses. The garden serves 350 Latino families and 1,000 community residents weekly and we are fighting for it.

The Belmont Art Park, which has been used as open space for many years, is threatened with development

Each and every project is unique a serves an interesting part of public life in L.A.

From urban farm plots to jogging paths to a civic park downtown, we need open space to create community.

The City of Los Angeles needs to create a park at the old Caltrans site, as a strong project to revitalize Downtown L.A. for its residents. This site is the perfect site for a park because it is framed by three of L.A. ’s most important institutions, City Hall, the L.A. Times and Caltrans.

All over the U.S. people are moving back into urban centers and enjoying the benefits of urban life. New Yorkers love their city, not for their housing but for their access to public life.
Compared to San Francisco or New York, Los Angeles is the last big city to experience this urban renaissance.

However, many new residents of downtown L.A. discover that it does not have basic amities of urban living and many end up moving out after only a year or so.

What draws people to urban centers are other people. A great civic park can become the living room for downtown Los Angeles residents. Here we can come together to meet our neighbors –
including writers for the L.A. Times writers, Caltrans engineers and city employees.

Without adequate open space we stay in our apartments and lofts or drive to other places to engage in social activities.

Disney Concert Hall alone cannot revitalize Downtown L.A. . Each and every one of us can make a difference through our daily activities. Pete’s Café is one of the best amenities serving downtown L.A. residents because it adds life to the streets after 5 p.m. Gallery Row is another example of successful urban programming in L.A. .

As residents, developers and visitors to downtown L.A. , we need to make our vision loud and clear.

We need to convince residents from Chatsworth to Boyle Heights that they need this park too and that they can enjoy it when they come into the city center.

We need to convince every environmental group that this park will help stop urban sprawl.

We need to convince every housing advocate that this park will help make dense housing projects enjoyable.

We need to convince every housing developer investing in downtown that this park will enhance their investment by providing a great public amenity.

We need to convince our elected officials that this is our vision for the future of Los Angeles. Creating this park needs to become an issue in the mayoral race.

Little Tokyo residents convinced the City that 1st and Alameda was not a good place the police
headquarters and won their fight. They recommended that the police headquarters stay at its current site, which I support.

Creation of this park begins now with the democratic process. We need to form the parade and public and elected officials will follow.
