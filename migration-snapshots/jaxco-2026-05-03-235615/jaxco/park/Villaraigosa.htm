LACIVICPARK.ORG           6-28-05

 

 

To:  Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa

Re:  Plans for the LAPD headquarters

 

 

Dear Mayor Villaraigosa,

 

As you launch your new administration, we hope that an issue that will earn your early attention is a re-evaluation of the proposal for rebuilding Parker Center.  We believe the plan to build the new LAPD headquarters on the old Caltrans site and a parking garage and motor pool on the St. Vibiana’s block is deeply flawed. Not only does it squander the ideal site for a major civic park for Los Angeles, it makes terrible sense economically.

 

Those who chose these sites thought they were making a sound bottom-line decision. But their analysis left out many important considerations, including these:

 

  1. Loss of tax revenue. This proposal will in effect knock off the tax rolls two key blocks in the heart of downtown.  On the old Caltrans block, in conjunction with the park, commercially active pavilions and restaurants and a major underground public parking facility had been proposed -- all of which would generate revenue for the city. On the St. Vibiana’s block, three existing businesses -- employing 20 people -- would be torn down. These and additional parcels on the block which are prime for residential and commercial development would be forever taken out of the private, tax-generating sector.

 

  1. Fate of the Parker Center site:  Under the current plan, the Parker Center property is left in limbo.  Because the jail and the emergency call center are located there, it is unsuitable for residential or commercial development. And we understand that the LAPD believes this site to be inappropriate for any non-police use – including city offices.  What is the benefit of yet another abandoned site in the city center? Why not make the best possible use of it by rebuilding Parker Center at its current location?  The city has presumed that it would be too expensive to temporarily relocate the LAPD while a new center is built.  In reality, the cost of doing so would be an investment in giving the LAPD the best new home possible and serve the larger goal of building a vibrant downtown.  An option that has not been fully explored is also one that could substantially reduce the interim costs:  keep all or part of the just-renovated (at a cost of $7.5 million) Parker Center operational while the new facility is built around it.

 

  1. Cost of buying more property: The city already owns so many properties it has been unable to even catalog them all.  Among its properties are several purchased in previous misguided attempts to relocate the LAPD headquarters.  How does one justify to taxpayers spending even more city money on the purchase of even more land when we face so many pressing issues – including putting more police officers on the streets.

 

  1. Legal issues.  In assessing the cost of the project, the city should anticipate legal challenges to the current proposal.  It ignores previous and long-standing plans for both blocks, adds environmental dangers and congestion, displaces businesses and challenges the rights of private property owners.  In addition to the substantial costs of defending and or mitigating these very real underlying issues, the time involved in resolving them could set back by years plans for the much-needed new LAPD headquarters.

 

  1. Arts as a resource.  Crushing the arts endeavors organically occurring on the St. Vibiana’s block will be a costly mistake. The creative, artistic spirit found on this block is precisely what has been identified as a critical component in the rebirth of city centers all across the nation. This block is home to the landmark 1876 St. Vibiana’s, newly restored as a performing arts center and which would be boxed in by the proposed police facilities. The block is home to the 1896 building where Gallery Row was founded and where the MJ Higgins gallery now thrives. The gallery building is one of three on the block that would be torn down to make way for a police-only parking garage, car wash and gas station. Across the street, the historic Linda Lea Theatre, about to be reclaimed as a film and arts center, would no longer face an art gallery, but rather a police car wash.  These arts and cultural projects are the result of a synergy and hard-found energy that only springs from the ground up.  The city needs to nurture them, not dismiss them as irrelevant.

 

  1. Park as catalyst:  A park on the old Caltrans site, as envisioned by city planners and as called for in the master plan for Civic Center development, would serve as a catalyst for sustainable, healthy development in the Historic Core and other adjacent communities. A park is an amenity that the city can champion. Making downtown a place people want to live and visit requires multiple connected successes, not isolated projects. A park on the old Caltrans site and a lively arts community on the St. Vibiana’s block will go a long way toward creating a positive identity for downtown – one that honors its history and nurtures its civic life. And they may well be a critical component in ensuring the success of the nearby Grand Avenue project. A park on the old Caltrans site will connect the project to rest of the city via 1st and 2nd streets and to downtown population centers in a way redevelopment of the mall between City Hall and Grand Avenue cannot do on its own.  While a vital component of a city’s infrastructure, police facilities would have the opposite effect if placed on these blocks.

 

Our grassroots coalition, lacivicpark.org, was formed to help get plans for a major park on the old Caltrans site back on track. More than 1,500 people have signed our petition in support of the park.

 

Among those who have studied the city’s plans and been troubled by them are members of the city’s Cultural Affairs Commission, who have twice voted against approving the LAPD headquarters plans and have sent a letter to the City Council urging it to reconsider.  The Arts and Cultural and the Land Use and Planning committees of the Downtown LA Neighborhood Council have adopted motions opposing all or part of the plan. So have Gallery Row and the Residents Assn. of Downtown LA.   DLANC adopted a resolution this month formally opposing plans for a garage and motor pool on the St. Vibiana’s block. 

 

People are objecting to these plans because they are not the solution the City Council thought it had when it made its decision last June to endorse them.

 

The time to stop and rethink this is now – before more time and money is lost.  With your new leadership, we believe the city can devise a plan that serves both the LAPD and the city at large for generations to come. It is significant that the city formally and professionally studied this very issue less than 10 years ago.  The master plan for Civic Center redevelopment – adopted by the city and county in 1997 – envisions rebuilding Parker Center on its current site and envisions the old Caltrans site as a civic park. 

 

Creating a major park on this site is truly a once-in-a-century opportunity to symbolically and materially change Los Angeles for the better. And while we believe that doing so makes good economic sense, it’s not something just to be measured in dollars.  It is about establishing inclusive priorities – and capturing the imagination of citizens by creating a welcoming place for them in the center of the city.

 

Sincerely yours,

On behalf of lacivicpark.org
