MAIN STREET SOUTH OF ST VIBIANA'S
Just what we need along the east side of Calle Principal: an eight foot high
concrete block wall and Ficus trees around a police motor pool set for the
balance of the block immediately south of St. Vibiana's - now being
preserved and complexified by Gilmore Associates. WONDERFULL.

Quite deadly to street life, folks. Are you listening, Councilwoman Perry?
How about it, Sue Laris? It wasn't so long ago that your paper was extolling
the newfound virtues of 'Urban Design' in Downtown Los Angeles.

A bit of civic memory to consider before urbanicide is stamped, approved and
forever imposed upon the east side of Main, from the Third Street
intersection on up to the old cathedral: As one proceeds north -
The site of the W.S. Hancock Residence - Los Angeles' haunting connection to
Pickett's Charge at The Battle of Gettysburg (and one of the greater legends
of America's Civil War). Read Michael Shaara's Pulitzer Prize winning novel
'The Killer Angels' or rent the movie 'Gettysburg.' Next there is and
remains the remainder of the Wonderland Theater, the first theater
constructed in the West expressly for motion pictures, 1910. A.C. Martin was
the architect. The front was removed and reconfigured in 1931 so that East
Third and West Third could connect with no offset. Visit Brent C.
Dickerson's website and have a
look at old Main Street and what this theater looked like at the end of the
Roaring Twenties.
Next there was the two story Ponet Block, designed in the summer of 1901 by
Morgan and Walls. This parcel is now a parking lot. Here it was that the
city's very first brick building, a "dwelling house", was constructed in
1853 by Capt. Jesse Hunter. Two years later, future mayor and state senator
Cameron Thom bought it.
In late 1860 he constructed a duplicate of his residence to the south, where
the civil war legend, as legend be, would take place in June of the
following year. Thom's residence was demolished to allow for construction
the Ponet Block. Wait: that's not all. When the Ponet Block was finished in
March of 1902, the center commercial space was leased to former cowboy
Thomas Lincoln Tally and the next month there was opened the very first
cinema in the western hemisphere. Thom was not to be mentioned by Shaara;
never-the-less, he too was present in battle on the third day, fighting with
Lewis Armistead and Richard Garnett against Hancock - "The Hero of
Gettysburg" and future Democratic candidate for president (1880, the closest
popular vote for president in America's history). All four of them had
gathered together at a 1861 farewell party, organized by Mrs. Hancock, as
legend has had it.
Fifteen years earlier and immediately north of the Ponet Block there was
constructed the Tivoli Theater. It extended two thirds of the way back to
Los Angeles Street and had an oppulent entry garden, as recalled by Harris
Newmark - "the Samuel Pepys of Los Angeles" - who formerly had lived in an
adobe directly across the street. A year or so after the theater opened, the
city's visionary fathers decided that there must be a mid-block alley and
therewith, the venue was demolished in order that progress reign.
Immediately north of this, Newmark had constructed his own three story
commercial block; the bottom floor still remains.
The two upper floors housed the Metropolitan Hotel. There was also the
Dresden Hotel in the second floor of the Schwarz and Hoffman block further
to the north and in 1895, the International Order of OddFellows Hall was
constructed on the south side of the cathedral. Many years later, OddFellows
became the Union Resue Mission seven full decades after the very first
rescue mission had been organized over across on the west side of Main.

Now, all of this history is slated to be replaced by glorious concrete block
manufactured out in Sun Valley. Please city fathers, don't tell us about a
landscaped setback. Just go and look at the glorious L.A. Times parking
garage and the hideous impact that THAT frontage has had on both Broadway
and Spring. Interesting that it was generated by the very same architect who
gave us our gorgeous World Trade Center. If this all fails to register, then
consider taking a stroll down Flower - from Third to Fifth and open your
eyes folks. Hopefully it ain't too late.
