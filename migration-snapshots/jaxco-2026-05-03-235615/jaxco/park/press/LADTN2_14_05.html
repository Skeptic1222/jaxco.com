Los Angeles Downtown News
Feb. 14, 2005

Time to Rethink Civic Square
City Council Should Back Cultural Affairs Commission's Gutsy Decision

by Sam Hall Kaplan

The Los Angeles City Council has been given a rare opportunity to correct a grievous decision to locate the new police headquarters on a prime Downtown site. From a planning perspective, the land demands to be a park.

The city's Cultural Affairs Commission should be thanked, instead of being castigated, for exceeding its authority. On Feb. 3 the panel, at a hearing meant to discuss the proposed building's design, instead listened to a crowd of angry area stakeholders and requested that the Council reconsider the use of the site bounded by First, Second, Spring and Main streets. They did so despite warnings during the meeting that the Council would likely overturn their decision.

Located at the approximate intersection of the Historic Core, Little Tokyo, Bunker Hill and the Civic Center, directly south of City Hall, the block begs to be the focal point and people place of an emergent Downtown, not the site of a security-encrusted fortress.

Call it what you will. And no matter how styled and attractively landscaped, a police headquarters does not generate a welcoming public presence. With its high volume of squad car traffic in an already congested area, it really should be located adjacent to a freeway, perhaps north of the 101, and east of the MTA complex.

The First Street site occupied at present by the former Caltrans office building had been selected by the Council for the proposed 500,000-square-foot police structure because it frankly was the quickest and least expensive alternative to replacing the current earthquake-compromised headquarters a block away.

Frugality and expediency are always welcomed in government, but not in this instance, when they corrupt a widely praised and inspired vision for the site proposed in the Civic Center Shared Facilities and Enhancement Plan. Not incidentally, it was approved by the Council in 1997. The site also was designated and labeled the "Civic Square" in a subsequent more-focused plan prepared by the short-lived Los Angeles Civic Center Authority.

Why have these publicized planning exercises - involving paid professionals as well as a conscientious public - if they are not to be used as guides to the promise of a more livable city? In the case of Los Angeles, that long-held dream includes an attractive central city.

As for that dream, much has been made of late of the billions of dollars in public and private investment flowing into Downtown, with a special focus on a host of notable new buildings. These include the sculptural Walt Disney Concert Hall, the more subdued Cathedral of Our Lady of the Angels and the high-tech, high-profile Caltrans Headquarters. Soon to be unveiled is the proposed Grand Avenue development that will no doubt feature a cluster of sparkling, pricey towers.

Iconic buildings may make for great photo ops, but not great cities. Great places, however, offer open, accessible, people-gathering points. They are places to sit, celebrate, perhaps to protest or enjoy a passing parade.

The Civic Square could be such a place, drawing users out of the nearby government offices, the increasing residential population of the adjacent Old Bank District, and tourists from Bunker Hill and beyond. It also even might attract reporters and editors out from the insular Los Angeles Times building to the west. Unlike Chicago or Seattle, L.A.'s benign climate invites experiencing a city beyond exchanging secondhand reports over PCs or in corporate dining rooms. Eating al fresco in a park would be a start for the Times staff.

One should also keep in mind Vibiana Place. With its proposed public library, performing arts center, apartments and boutique hotel, it will fit well with an inviting park diagonally across the street, rather than face a forbidding police headquarters. It is bad enough that the mixed-use project already has the new Caltrans headquarters hovering to the north. Another such monolith could really damage its viability.

As for Caltrans, it apparently was designed to take advantage of an open park such as the Civic Square to its west, so as to lessen the bulk that many feel is out of scale. If the 11-story police headquarters is instead located there, the resulting facade along First Street will be daunting and depressing.

Now that the Council will have to take up the site issue again, it would be interesting to hear the recommendations of the design team studying First Street from Bunker Hill to East L.A. When the participating firms were hired by Project Restore, the Police Headquarters was a given.

That obviously has changed, for the design team, as well as for Downtown community activists, park advocates and the City Council.

Kaplan is an Emmy Award-winning reporter for FOX 11 and the author of L.A. Lost and Found.
