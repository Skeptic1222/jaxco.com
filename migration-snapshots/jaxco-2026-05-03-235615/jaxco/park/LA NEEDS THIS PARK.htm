LA NEEDS THIS PARK

For more than a decade, a great park has been envisioned on the block just south of City Hall - at 1st and Main, 2nd and Spring. The park has been derailed by the abrupt decision of the LA City Council to build a police headquarters on the site. We think plans for this park need to be put back on track. Here's why:

We have a historic opportunity to give the citizens of LA a place in the city's civic center.
Public parks and civic gathering places are hallmarks of great cities but Los Angeles has grown up with almost none. This block - site of the old Caltrans building and about to become the property of the people of Los Angeles - gives us a rare opportunity to dramatically change that. Urban planners and city officials have repeatedly identified this block as ideal for a great public space. Creating a park here would extend a long-overdue welcome to residents of this far-flung city, so in need of common ground. At the steps of City Hall, this block is the public's gateway to its government and it is the city's gateway to its historic core, business districts and emerging residential and arts corridors. Bordering it are LA's earliest thoroughfares and some of its oldest and newest landmarks -- City Hall, the LA Times, St. Vibiana's, the new Caltrans building, and, just up the street, Disney Hall. How this block is used will in many ways define the kind of city we become.

The proposed LAPD headquarters would dominate two critical blocks in the city center.
The proposed LAPD headquarters will not just take over the old Caltrans site, but much of the adjacent block on Main Street anchored by St. Vibiana's. The combination will permanently alter the potential of the area to develop into a commercial and pedestrian friendly zone and it will leave almost no public parking in the area. On the block directly across from City Hall would be the 11-story, 500,000-square-foot headquarters, an auditorium, parking for 700 police vehicles and a small community park. Fronting Main, in the block running south of St. Vibiana's, would be parking for another 500 police vehicles and a motor pool where police cars are washed and serviced.

A park here would support the new arts movements on Gallery Row and at St. Vibiana's.
The old Caltrans block marks the start of Gallery Row, an arts initiative along Main and Spring streets that in less than a year has grown from 4 galleries to 15. Works of art, arts fairs and festivals in the park were to have played a key role in bringing people into the area. Under the city's current plan, not only is there no park to showcase artwork and attract crowds, the building on Main Street where the gallery row concept was born would be torn down to make way for the police motor pool and parking. Next to those facilities would be one of the most historic buildings downtown, St. Vibiana's, built in 1876. The former cathedral - narrowly saved from an earlier wrecking ball -- has just undergone a $6 million renovation and transformation into an arts center. Across the street is the old Linda Lea Theater, about to be renovated into a performance and specialty film venue. These endeavors, allowed to thrive, will help create a lively, authentic arts district that bridges the established art museums just east and west -- MOCA, the Geffen Contemporary and the Japanese American National Museum - and the Music Center's performing arts venues up the hill.

Workers, residents and downtown's children need a healthy, welcoming park.
This site is close to thousands of downtown workers and a growing number of residents who need park space to help them lead healthy lives. In a densely populated area, we have a critical responsibility to ensure that there is park space where people can gather, jog, relax and breath a little easier - and where our children have a safe place to play. Two-thirds of Los Angeles children do not live within walking distance of a public park - the ratio is even worse downtown. Development of market rate and affordable housing downtown isn't just a trend, it's a necessity. Creating a park on this site would radically improve the environment for those who work and live in the surrounding districts and all who come to visit. This is a quality of life and health issue that we can no longer afford to ignore.

A park on this site will foster downtown businesses and residential development.
A great park on the old Caltrans site will help connect and strengthen established and developing business, arts and residential communities. Little Tokyo, the Historic Core, Artists, Toy, Broadway, Bunker Hill and Grand Avenue districts will all benefit if pedestrians and other visitors can move easily from one to the other. This site has the unique potential to help bring these interests together in creating a critical mass of arts venues, restaurants and shops. Although banners proclaim downtown as a place to live, work, and play, there is precious little place to meet or play. Human-friendly public spaces will help ensure the success of downtown redevelopment - and the tax revenue and other benefits it will bring the entire city. Many of the 8,000 new downtown housing units in the pipeline or under consideration are near this site.

All park sites are not created equal. The "other" civic park is no substitute.
The old Caltrans site is ideal for a park. In addition to its pivotal location, it is on level land and is open and welcoming from all sides. Redoing the space between the courts and other civic buildings between City Hall and the Music Center, as proposed in the Grand Avenue redevelopment plan, would create a welcome greenbelt, but not a working park. That site is hidden behind walls of government buildings that cut it off from surrounding streets and public view. It is not a naturally inviting place for people to gather because it is on a hillside. And, because most of it lies between courthouses and county offices, noise and security restrictions will almost certainly limit its public uses and appeal. The two projects are very different, but taken together, they can strengthen each other and help connect the redevelopment of Grand Avenue to the surrounding eclectic communities.

From wrong location to wrong location: It's time to find the right site for the LAPD.
The LA City Council listened when residents and businesses in Little Tokyo and the Artists District protested that the police headquarters at 1st and Alameda would disrupt renewal efforts in those communities. But then the council did something stunning: it voted to put the police facility on this even more sensitive site. City officials must develop a rational plan that gives the LAPD the headquarters it needs and supports rather than disrupts the important yet fragile urban renewal under way downtown. Decision makers need to pay attention to the long-range planning that has already taken place - including the master plan adopted by the City Council in 1997 that calls for open space on this block. There are alternatives for the new police headquarters - including rebuilding on the current Parker Center site. The sooner the right site is identified, the sooner genuine progress can be made.

A great civic park in downtown LA will be a point of pride for generations to come.
Backers of this park envision a place as extraordinary as this city. One concept shows it with a great artwork - a palm spiral envisioned by the late artist Robert Smithson. Others show a place where plants transform the space as seasons change, an urban waterscape, a hi-tech center for communicating ideas. Most see it with multiple uses, dynamic attractions and an underground parking facility to accommodate visitors. All see it as a place where people from LA's many and diverse communities can gather and enjoy their city.
